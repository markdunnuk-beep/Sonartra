# Single-Domain Editorial Style Guide

## 1. Purpose

This guide governs future editorial rewrites for the current single-domain assessment language assets.

It is the writing standard for the imported prose that feeds:

- Hero
- Drivers
- Pair
- Limitation
- Application

It applies across all signals and all pair combinations in the current single-domain assessment, not just the current local `results_process` example.

This guide is editorial only. It does not change scoring, payload shape, rendering, UI behaviour, or fallback logic.

## 2. Core Language Principles

Use these rules consistently across all section-owned prose.

- Write behaviourally, not as a personality label. Describe what the person is likely to do, notice, prioritise, or miss.
- Be specific, not generic. Name the practical pattern, not a vague leadership virtue.
- Stay practical, not motivational. The prose should help the reader interpret behaviour at work, not encourage or inspire them.
- Hold strength and tension together. Sonartra language should show what works and what it can cost when overused.
- Use probabilistic language, not absolutes. Prefer `likely`, `can`, `may`, `tends to`, `at times`, `when pressure rises`.
- Keep it concise. Each paragraph should carry a distinct idea and stop when the point is made.
- Avoid therapy and coaching clichés. Do not write in a reflective, healing, or self-help register.
- Avoid corporate filler. Cut phrases that sound polished but say little.
- Use British English throughout.

## 3. Section-by-Section Writing Rules

### Hero

Job of the section:

- name the clearest leadership pattern
- explain what kind of operating stance it creates
- introduce the core benefit and the first trade-off

Tone:

- clear
- confident
- interpretive
- grounded

Length:

- headline: short and distinct
- opening line: one direct sentence
- supporting paragraphs: usually two concise paragraphs

What to do:

- state what the pattern is strongest at
- show the operating logic behind the pattern
- introduce the first cost without becoming negative

What to avoid:

- generic praise
- repeating detailed driver claims
- naming two traits without explaining the pattern they create
- turning the Hero into a full development section

### Drivers

Job of the section:

- explain what is producing the pattern
- distinguish the role of each ranked signal clearly
- show why the signals are not doing the same job

Tone:

- diagnostic
- precise
- role-aware

Length:

- one strong paragraph per driver role
- long enough to explain the role, short enough to stay distinct from Pair

What to do:

- make each role sound meaningfully different
- describe mechanism, not just description
- explain how each signal shapes the visible pattern

What to avoid:

- interchangeable prose across roles
- repeating the Hero opening in different words
- generic signal descriptions that could fit any rank

### Pair

Job of the section:

- explain what the top two signals create together
- show the advantage of the combination
- show where the combination creates tension
- show what deliberate use looks like

Tone:

- synthetic
- balanced
- situational

Length:

- one opening paragraph plus two to three supporting paragraphs

What to do:

- explain interaction, not just two separate strengths
- show where the combination is especially useful
- name the friction created by the same combination

What to avoid:

- simply listing the two signals again
- writing the Pair section as a duplicate Hero
- making the tension sound like a character flaw

### Limitation

Job of the section:

- show where the current pattern narrows
- explain what gets over-emphasised
- identify the missing range constructively

Tone:

- candid
- constructive
- non-punitive

Length:

- one title plus two to three concise paragraphs

What to do:

- explain the cost of over-relying on the current pattern
- connect the limitation to real leadership conditions
- make the missing range feel useful, not moral

What to avoid:

- negative labelling
- shame-heavy language
- implying the strongest signal is bad
- writing limitation copy that sounds final or fixed

### Application

Job of the section:

- translate the reading into deliberate action
- tell the reader what to use, what to watch, and what to develop

Tone:

- practical
- behavioural
- direct

Length:

- each statement should work as a short actionable paragraph
- enough detail to be usable, not enough to become repetitive

What to do:

- write actions the reader can recognise in live work
- focus on observable choices, habits, and checks
- make each statement earn its place

What to avoid:

- motivational slogans
- vague advice like `be more self-aware`
- repeating the same instruction across Rely on, Notice, and Develop

## 4. Signal Role Rules

These roles must not sound interchangeable.

### Primary driver

Write as the signal setting the main operating emphasis.

It should answer:

- what this person most instinctively leads with
- what becomes most structurally visible in the pattern

Primary language should sound like the central force, not just a useful trait.

### Secondary driver

Write as the signal reinforcing or sharpening the pattern created by the primary driver.

It should answer:

- what strengthens the primary pattern
- how the pattern becomes more effective or more intense

Secondary language should not sound like another primary driver.

### Supporting context

Write as the signal adding range, perspective, or depth behind the leading pattern.

It should answer:

- what sits behind the visible pattern
- what broadens or contextualises the leading emphasis

Supporting language should feel quieter but still meaningful. It should not read like filler.

### Range limitation

Write as the least available range under pressure or habit.

It should answer:

- what is least likely to lead the pattern
- what the person may notice too late
- what wider range would add

Range limitation language should not sound punitive. It should identify missing range, not a personal defect.

## 5. Pair Language Rules

Pair language should explain the combination as a working pattern.

Always cover four jobs:

- what the combination creates
- where the advantage appears
- where the tension appears
- what deliberate use looks like

Writing rules:

- describe interaction, not two parallel traits
- make clear which part of the combination creates momentum, structure, pressure, range, or friction
- show why the pair is useful in a real situation
- show how the same pair can narrow the pattern if overused
- end with the most deliberate version of the pair, not a motivational flourish

Avoid:

- `X plus Y means...` with no practical explanation
- trait stacking
- generic statements that could fit many pairs
- identical paragraph structure across every pair

## 6. Application Language Rules

### Rely on

Purpose:

- strengths to use deliberately

Write:

- what this pattern does well when used well
- where it creates value
- how to use the strength without overextending it

Avoid:

- pure praise
- advice that belongs in Notice or Develop

### Notice

Purpose:

- early warning signs

Write:

- what drift looks like before it becomes a larger issue
- what starts narrowing, hardening, or getting missed
- what the reader should catch earlier

Avoid:

- alarmist or punitive phrasing
- generic risk statements with no observable signal

### Develop

Purpose:

- next behavioural focus

Write:

- what deliberate expansion would look like
- how to strengthen range or effectiveness
- what practical question, habit, or behaviour should be added

Avoid:

- abstract self-improvement language
- advice so broad that it could fit any result

## 7. Before / After Examples

These are generic examples only. They are not live CSV rewrites.

### Hero

Weak:

- `You are a strong and effective leader who gets things done.`

Stronger:

- `You are likely to lead by turning ambiguity into a clearer route for action, especially when the work needs structure more than further discussion.`

### Drivers

Weak:

- `This signal is important to how you lead.`

Stronger:

- `This signal shapes the pattern by making you notice where the work lacks sequence, ownership, or a dependable standard.`

### Secondary driver

Weak:

- `This signal also matters a lot.`

Stronger:

- `This signal reinforces the pattern by adding pace, pressure, or sharper judgement about what progress should look like.`

### Supporting context

Weak:

- `This signal is present too.`

Stronger:

- `This signal sits behind the pattern as added range, helping the work connect to a wider purpose rather than only the immediate demand.`

### Range limitation

Weak:

- `You are bad at this signal.`

Stronger:

- `This is the range least likely to lead when pressure rises, which means its value may need to be brought in more deliberately.`

### Pair

Weak:

- `Together these two signals make you balanced.`

Stronger:

- `Together these signals create a pattern that can move work forward with unusual clarity, but can also narrow the field if pace and control harden before wider commitment is built.`

### Limitation

Weak:

- `Your weakness is that you overdo things.`

Stronger:

- `The cost appears when the same strengths that create traction start to narrow what gets noticed, especially when the human conditions around the work need more attention than the system itself.`

### Application

Weak:

- `Try to be more aware of others.`

Stronger:

- `Before locking the route, check who understands the plan, who trusts it, and who is carrying cost without yet saying so.`

## 8. Editing Guardrails

Editors may rewrite:

- prose fields
- headlines
- `claim_text`
- application statements

Editors must not change:

- `domain_key`
- `pair_key`
- `signal_key`
- `driver_role`
- `claim_type`
- `materiality`
- `priority`
- any structural enum
- any structural key

If a field controls routing, grouping, role ownership, ranking, or import validation, it is not editorial prose.

## 9. Quality Checklist

Use this checklist before importing revised language.

- Hero does not duplicate the Drivers section.
- Each driver role has a distinct job and distinct tone.
- Primary, secondary, supporting, and range-limitation copy do not sound interchangeable.
- Pair language explains interaction, not just two traits placed side by side.
- Limitation language is constructive, not negative or moralising.
- Application language is actionable and behaviourally specific.
- No paragraph relies on fallback-only assumptions.
- No UI-only labels are written into CSV prose.
- No sentence depends on structural keys being rewritten.
- British English is used throughout.
- Strength and tension are both present where the section requires them.
- The prose is concise enough to scan, but specific enough to be useful.
