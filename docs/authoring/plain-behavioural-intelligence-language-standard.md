# Plain Behavioural Intelligence Language Standard

The Sonartra reader-first authoring model uses plain behavioural intelligence: language that helps a reader recognise how a pattern works in real life, why it matters, and what to do with it.

## Core Standard

### Plain behavioural intelligence

Write about observable behaviour, choices, attention, energy, habits, and trade-offs. Avoid vague trait labels unless they are immediately explained in practical terms.

### Immediate clarity

The reader should understand the point on first read. Use short sentences where possible. Put the main behavioural point before nuance.

### Recognition

Language should help the reader think, "Yes, I can see how that shows up." Recognition matters more than impressive phrasing.

### Behavioural specificity

Name concrete behaviours: what the person tends to notice, start, avoid, protect, overuse, postpone, seek, or repeat. Do not rely on abstract nouns.

### Practical usefulness

Every section should help the reader interpret or apply the pattern. Include useful implications, not just description.

### Calm authority

Sound grounded, measured, and confident. Avoid hype, diagnosis, judgement, or exaggerated certainty.

### Editorial polish last

First make the sentence true, clear, and useful. Then improve rhythm, flow, and elegance. Do not polish unclear thinking.

### Avoid abstract premium fog

Do not use elevated but empty phrases such as "unlocking potential", "transformational clarity", "optimised alignment", or "high-impact self-actualisation" unless the behaviour is made specific.

### Smart 13-year-old readability with adult tone

Use language a smart 13-year-old could understand, while keeping the tone adult, respectful, and professional.

## Practical Checks

- Would a normal reader know what this means without rereading?
- Does it describe behaviour rather than a vague trait?
- Does it balance strength and risk without sounding negative?
- Does it help the reader do something with the insight?
- Could the sentence appear in a serious professional report without sounding inflated?
