## 5. Full-Set Strengths

The set is readable. Most reports use short sentences, plain paragraphs, and practical workplace language.

The reports are reader-first. They explain how the reader tends to decide rather than describing an internal model.

The signal model is preserved. Evidence means facts and what can be checked. Judgement means professional read and experience. Standards means responsibility and what people can stand behind. Practicality means useful action and what will work.

Role-neutral workplace relevance is present across all 24 reports. Every report contains a workplace example that demonstrates the rank sequence.

Section structure is consistent. All reports use the Decision Style labels and stable IDs.

Development actions are one of the strongest full-set assets. They are concrete and usually include useful review questions.

The set avoids obvious personality-test tone. It does not lean on archetype claims in body copy, even when some titles still sound type-like.

The reports avoid runtime or scoring language. They do not introduce score-shape variants, thresholds, overlays, or model-facing explanations.

The set is ready for title normalisation because body-level signal logic is sufficiently stable. RFA-20A can now compare all 24 title/body/opening relationships.

## 6. Full-Set Risks

Repeated opening structures are the main body-copy risk. Many reports open with "Your decision style is..." followed by "You usually start..." This is plain and effective, but the full set needs more variation before final compilation.

Title collisions are the main title-system risk. The same title appears in different families: `The Practical Evidence Decision Maker` and `The Practical Experience Decision Maker` are duplicated across distinct rank orders. Several other titles are near-duplicates.

"Decider" vs "Decision Maker" is inconsistent. The final set should almost certainly normalise toward "Decision Maker" unless there is a deliberate exception.

Family anchor words are overused:

- Evidence-led: facts, evidence, known, check
- Judgement-led: experience, read, notice
- Standards-led: responsible, fair, stand behind
- Practicality-led: action, practical, response, move forward

Role-neutral examples are useful but structurally repetitive. Many use the "For example, imagine a team..." construction and then walk through the four signals in order.

Section 10 Strengths is often too generic. It is accurate, but it can read like a ranked-signal recap rather than distinctive value.

Section 15 Closing is the most formulaic section across the set. Many closings use the same rhythm: "You start with X because decisions need Y."

Later batches are leaner. Standards-led and Practicality-led reports average several hundred words less than the benchmark and Evidence-led family. They still pass structure, but some feel less premium.

Rank 4 is usually framed as quieter, but some reports still lean toward "late" or "delayed." Final polish should make rank 4 positively useful, not merely a risk.

Some titles sound archetypal or mechanical: "Experience-to-Action," "Experience-and-Responsibility," "Responsible Practical Judgement," and "Experience-Shaped Action."

Cross-family blur happens when rank 2 dominates the reader's impression. Evidence-second Practicality reports can sound Evidence-led by title. Standards-second Practicality reports can sound Standards-led. Practicality-second Judgement reports can sound Practicality-led.

## 7. Signal Accuracy Audit

### Evidence

Evidence works well across rank positions. When rank 1, it clearly means facts before commitment. When rank 2, it checks a professional read, responsibility concern, or practical answer. When rank 3, it gives support and correction. When rank 4, it usually still has power to challenge the first answer.

The strongest Evidence handling appears in the benchmark, `evidence_judgement_standards_practicality`, `judgement_evidence_practicality_standards`, and `practicality_judgement_standards_evidence`.

The main risk is lower-ranked Evidence becoming "confirmation" after the decision already feels right. The Practicality-led and Standards-led reports mostly avoid this by asking what facts could prove the answer wrong.

Reports needing attention: `standards_judgement_practicality_evidence`, `practicality_standards_judgement_evidence`, and `judgement_practicality_standards_evidence` should preserve Evidence as a real corrective test during polish.

### Judgement

Judgement is handled professionally across the set. It is described as experience, professional read, noticing patterns, likely effects, people, timing, pressure, repeated behaviour, and context.

The reports generally avoid guessing, instinct, vibe, mood, impulse, or unsupported feeling. This is a major success for the set.

The main improvement is specificity. Some passages rely on "experience tells you" without always naming what experience is noticing. The strongest passages name handoffs, adoption, strain, side effects, timing, pressure, workload, or trust.

Reports needing attention: `judgement_standards_evidence_practicality`, `judgement_standards_practicality_evidence`, `practicality_judgement_evidence_standards`, and `standards_judgement_practicality_evidence` should add more concrete "what the reader notices" language during polish.

### Standards

Standards is consistently responsibility-led. It usually means fairness, consistency, trust, consequences, defensibility, commitments, and what people can stand behind.

The reports avoid moral superiority, rigidity, bureaucracy, compliance-only thinking, and perfectionism. Standards is not made into a scold.

The main risk is repetition. "Responsible," "fair," and "stand behind" appear often enough that Standards can feel generic in later batches. Final polish should add more concrete responsibility language: precedent, expectations, commitments, workload, accountability, uneven impact, and consequences people will live with.

Reports needing attention: `standards_practicality_evidence_judgement`, `standards_practicality_judgement_evidence`, `practicality_standards_evidence_judgement`, and `practicality_standards_judgement_evidence` need title/body clarity so Standards does not dominate or disappear in the wrong place.

### Practicality

Practicality is generally useful action, not speed. It means workable response, clear next step, implementation, realistic option, progress, and action under constraints.

When Practicality is lower-ranked, it sometimes carries less positive value than it should. It can read as "the next step may arrive late" rather than "this signal turns a careful decision into something usable."

The strongest Practicality handling appears in the benchmark, `evidence_practicality_standards_judgement`, `judgement_practicality_evidence_standards`, `standards_practicality_evidence_judgement`, and the Practicality-led family.

Reports needing attention: `evidence_judgement_standards_practicality`, `standards_evidence_judgement_practicality`, and `standards_judgement_evidence_practicality` should strengthen Practicality as positively useful when it is rank 4.
