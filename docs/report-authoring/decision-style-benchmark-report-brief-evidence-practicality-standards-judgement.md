# Decision Style Benchmark Report Brief — Evidence / Practicality / Standards / Judgement

## 1. Purpose

This brief defines the benchmark direction for the first fully authored Decision Style report.

It is not the full report, not a workbook/import task, and not a runtime implementation task. It sets the interpretation, editorial standard, section strategy, tonal discipline, and quality bar for later report authoring.

The future full report for `evidence_practicality_standards_judgement` should become the benchmark for scaling the remaining 23 Decision Style reports. It should prove that the clean report-first model can support a premium, pattern-specific canonical report without score-shape variants, modular worksheet assembly, thresholds, archetypes, overlays, sentence-library logic, WPLP carryover, multi-domain modelling, or runtime AI prose.

## 3. Core Pattern Thesis

This reader tends to trust what can be known first. They then look for the most workable route forward, using standards as a later check on whether the choice remains defensible. Their experience-led judgement is present, but it may come later, remain implicit, or be underused when the evidence-and-practicality route feels strong enough.

Plain-English version: this person wants the decision to make sense, lead somewhere usable, and still hold up when tested. They may need to name their experienced read sooner, especially when not everything important can be proven yet.

Premium report-style version: your decision pattern is built around a credible movement from known reality to practical commitment. You ground the choice in what can be observed or tested, translate that into a usable path, and then check whether the path remains defensible. The quieter development edge is to let experienced judgement enter earlier, before proof and workability have already narrowed the field.

Not this: this is not a cold, robotic, spreadsheet-only style; it is not shallow execution; it is not bureaucratic compliance; and it is not a lack of judgement. The pattern is a disciplined evidence-to-action sequence with a quieter tacit-read layer.

Avoid making Evidence sound emotionally detached, Practicality sound expedient, Standards sound bureaucratic, or Judgement sound like guesswork.

## 4. Signal Role Logic

### Rank 1 — Evidence

Evidence is the first trust point. The reader looks first for facts, proof, observed patterns, reliable data, or something that can be checked before commitment starts to build. This gives the decision credibility because the first move is anchored in known reality rather than preference, pressure, or persuasion.

Evidence gives this pattern its grounding. The reader wants the decision to rest on something observable, checkable, or defensible before momentum builds.

### Rank 2 — Practicality

Practicality is the shaping and translating move. It moves the reader from knowing to doing by asking what can be done with the available information, where the path is workable, and how progress can be made without waiting for perfect certainty.

Practicality turns the evidence into a route. It asks what can be done with what is known, rather than waiting for perfect certainty. It prevents Evidence from becoming endless analysis by forcing the question of usable movement.

### Rank 3 — Standards

Standards is the balancing check. Once a workable route is forming, Standards asks whether the choice remains consistent, responsible, defensible, and aligned with obligations or commitments that should not be eroded by speed or convenience.

Standards arrives as a quality-of-choice check: not only can this work, but is it still the kind of choice the reader can stand behind?

### Rank 4 — Judgement

Judgement is the underused or delayed perspective. Experience, tacit pattern recognition, contextual reading, and informed instinct may be present, but they can remain quieter than the evidence or the practical path. The reader may be more comfortable naming what can be shown than naming what they have learned to recognise.

Judgement may be quieter in this pattern. The reader may trust evidence and workable routes more readily than their own experienced read of what is emerging. When Judgement is left too late, the decision can miss contextual nuance, human signals, or the early pattern recognition that comes from lived experience.

## 5. Reader Recognition

The report should help this reader recognise that they are not simply cautious or analytical. They are trying to make decisions that can be explained, acted on, and defended.

Likely recognition points:

- "I want the decision to make sense before I move."
- "I am not trying to analyse forever; I want the facts to lead somewhere usable."
- "I can usually explain why I chose the route I chose."
- "I may discount a judgement call if I cannot yet show the evidence behind it."
- "People may trust my reasoning but sometimes want me to name the human or experiential read sooner."

What they may be proud of:

- They reduce guesswork.
- They create practical clarity from ambiguous information.
- They help others see why a route is credible.
- They usually avoid decisions that sound compelling but cannot be supported.

What they may not realise:

- They can close around a usable path before fully naming the standards or contextual judgement involved.
- They may treat subjective experience as less legitimate than observable proof.
- Others may trust their reasoning but still need to hear the experienced read, not only the evidence trail.

How others may experience them:

- Reasoned, clear, measured, useful, and credible.
- Occasionally too focused on what can be shown and done, especially when others are asking for contextual, relational, or standards-based interpretation.

The "yes, that is me" moment should come from the sequence: known reality first, practical path second, defensibility check third, quieter experienced read fourth. Avoid overusing "data-driven", reducing the pattern to spreadsheet thinking, or implying the reader lacks judgement or intuition.

## 6. Workplace Value

This pattern creates Decision value by improving decision quality, not by making broad claims about the reader's total organisational contribution.

The workplace value is:

- reliable grounding before commitment
- practical translation of information into action
- reduced guesswork in ambiguous but operationally real decisions
- credibility with stakeholders who need to understand why a route was chosen
- defensibility when choices are questioned later
- useful movement when the answer is incomplete but work still has to proceed

This reader is valuable when a decision needs both credibility and movement. They can take a messy field of information, identify what can be trusted, and build a path that others can follow.

## 7. Likely Blind Spots / Tightening Risks

Frame all risks as narrowing, not flaws.

Possible tightening risks:

- waiting for enough evidence even when experience already recognises the pattern
- closing around the most workable route before fully considering standards or obligations
- explaining decisions through facts and feasibility while under-explaining judgement
- underusing tacit knowledge, context, or human read
- treating subjective judgement as less legitimate than observable proof
- using Standards as a later audit rather than an earlier design constraint

The report must not make Evidence sound bad, Practicality sound expedient, Standards sound restrictive, or Judgement sound vague or mystical. The risk is not that the pattern is wrong; the risk is that it can narrow too quickly around what is known and usable before the full decision field has been interpreted.

## 11. Tone and Style Rules

The eventual report should sound:

- premium
- specific
- adult and professional
- commercially useful
- non-judgemental
- calm, precise, and direct
- grounded in workplace decisions rather than personality labels
- practical without becoming simplistic
- reflective without becoming therapy-coded

Avoid:

- personality-test generic phrasing
- overly academic explanation
- motivational fluff
- "data-driven" cliches
- "gut feeling" cliches
- making the reader sound emotionally detached
- treating lower-ranked signals as defects
- implying that a more intuitive style would be more mature

The tone should help a senior professional recognise the pattern, trust the analysis, and immediately see how to use it in real decision contexts.

## 12. Anti-Repetition Rules

High-risk repeated words:

- evidence
- workable
- grounded
- decision
- judgement
- route
- defensible

Useful variations:

- observable reality
- known facts
- practical path
- usable route
- credible basis
- experienced read
- standards check
- commitment point
- next move
- decision field
- proof point
- real-world constraint
- quality-of-choice check
- contextual read
- responsible path

Repetition controls:

- Do not begin every section by restating the rank order.
- Use "known reality" and "credible basis" when Evidence has already been named.
- Use "usable path", "next move", and "practical commitment" when Practicality has already been named.
- Use "quality-of-choice check" or "what should still hold" when Standards has already been named.
- Use "experienced read", "tacit recognition", or "contextual sense" when Judgement has already been named.
- Keep the full sequence explanation mainly in Section 07; other sections should apply the pattern to a specific reader problem.

## 13. Quality Bar

Minimum score before scaling: **8.5/10**.

Target benchmark quality: **9.0/10+**.

The report fails if it:

- reads like generic decision-making advice
- repeats Evidence and Practicality without adding interpretation
- treats Judgement as weakness or irrational instinct
- treats Standards as bureaucracy
- lacks a clear pattern-recognition moment
- stays too abstract for workplace use
- sounds like a leadership or operations report rather than Decision Style
- is not sufficiently different from other evidence-led patterns
- gives development advice without first creating recognition

The report feels premium if it:

- gives the reader a specific and recognisable decision sequence
- makes all four signals useful without flattening rank differences
- gives practical workplace application without sounding procedural
- makes the lower-ranked Judgement signal constructive and legitimate
- distinguishes credibility, workability, defensibility, and contextual read
- has tight section boundaries with little repetition
- uses titles, examples, and cautions that could not be pasted into most other patterns

## 14. Pattern Differentiation Notes

### Compared with `evidence_standards_practicality_judgement`

One-line difference: `evidence_practicality_standards_judgement` moves from known reality into usable action before checking standards; `evidence_standards_practicality_judgement` checks obligations and defensibility before narrowing toward execution.

Must not be confused: do not make this benchmark sound like a compliance-first or principle-gated evidence pattern. Standards is important here, but it arrives after the workable path begins to form.

### Compared with `evidence_judgement_practicality_standards`

One-line difference: `evidence_practicality_standards_judgement` translates proof into action before naming the experienced read; `evidence_judgement_practicality_standards` pairs proof with pattern recognition before moving toward practical execution.

Must not be confused: do not give this benchmark too much early tacit interpretation. Judgement is present, but quieter and later.

### Compared with `practicality_evidence_standards_judgement`

One-line difference: `evidence_practicality_standards_judgement` starts with what is known and then asks what can work; `practicality_evidence_standards_judgement` starts with workable movement and uses evidence to support or correct that path.

Must not be confused: do not let Practicality become the first trust point. In this benchmark, action is shaped by evidence rather than evidence being gathered to support action.

### Compared with `standards_evidence_practicality_judgement`

One-line difference: `evidence_practicality_standards_judgement` starts from observable reality and practical path-building; `standards_evidence_practicality_judgement` starts from what should remain true and then gathers evidence to protect that standard.

Must not be confused: do not make standards the moral anchor of the whole report. Standards is a meaningful later check, not the opening lens.

## 15. Authoring Risks and Mitigations

- **Risk: Evidence and Practicality blur together.** Mitigation: keep Evidence as the credibility basis and Practicality as the translation into movement.
- **Risk: Standards arrives too late in the interpretation.** Mitigation: introduce Standards as a real quality-of-choice check in the thesis and section guidance, even though it is rank 3.
- **Risk: Judgement sounds like missing instinct.** Mitigation: describe Judgement as experienced read, tacit recognition, and contextual pattern reading, not guesswork.
- **Risk: The report becomes too operational.** Mitigation: keep the focus on how decisions are made, explained, tested, and matured, not on task execution.
- **Risk: The report underplays human/contextual read.** Mitigation: use Sections 06, 08, 12, and 14 to surface the human and experiential layer.
- **Risk: The report sounds like advice rather than recognition.** Mitigation: create strong self-recognition before development actions; use "you may recognise..." before "try...".
- **Risk: The report title sounds generic.** Mitigation: prefer a title that combines credibility and route-building rather than broad labels like "The Credible Decision Maker".
- **Risk: The report is too similar to Leadership Approach Results/Process language.** Mitigation: avoid mobilisation, delivery architecture, team leadership, and results/process framing; keep the language anchored to judgement sequence, decision quality, commitment, and defensibility.
