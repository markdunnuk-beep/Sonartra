Verdict: strong enough to proceed, minor polish recommended later.

The batch handles Judgement professionally. It mostly avoids making Judgement sound like guessing, unsupported feeling, or impulse. The reports are clear enough to use as canonical drafts and are structurally consistent with the Evidence-led batch.

The main weakness is title quality. The titles are serviceable but feel more archetypal and mechanical than the approved benchmark title. This should be captured for RFA-20A title normalisation rather than fixed now.

## 4. Batch-Level Strengths

The batch is readable and practical. The reports use plain sentences, concrete workplace situations, and direct development prompts. They are not written as theory or as internal authoring notes.

Judgement is handled professionally. The reports describe it as experience, professional read, noticing patterns, and recognising what is happening before everything is fully proven. They avoid the main prohibited framing around gut feeling, impulse, mood, and unsupported feeling.

Role-neutral examples are present in every report. The examples cover recurring issues, complaint patterns, sensitive performance issues, strained working relationships, same-day response, and before-end-of-day problems. This is more varied than the Evidence-led batch and is a useful improvement.

Section structure is consistent. The reports preserve the Decision Style section labels and IDs, including `key-insight`, `value`, `others`, `decisions`, `communication`, `pressure`, `strengths`, `tightening`, `rank-3-expansion`, `rank-4-expansion`, `development-focus`, and `closing`.

Development actions are usable. They give practical review questions such as "What practical step would test whether my read is right?", "Who is affected, and what would make this responsible?", and "What facts could change this choice?"

The batch avoids most abstract authoring language. It relies on reader-facing terms such as experience, professional read, facts, responsibility, action, response, and what can be done.

## 5. Batch-Level Risks

The reports can still sound similar because every report begins with Judgement. Several openings use variants of "Your decision style starts with what experience..." This is accurate, but RFA-17C should keep carrying forward the Evidence-led audit lesson: vary opening rhythm and make rank 2 the first real differentiator.

Judgement does not generally sound like guessing or unsupported intuition. The bigger risk is repetition of "experience" without always naming exactly what the reader notices. The best passages name timing, people, repeated behaviour, recurring issues, handoffs, complaint patterns, trust, or pressure. Future polish should increase that specificity.

Title quality is the clearest weakness. "The Experience-and-Responsibility Decider" sounds mechanical. "The Experience-to-Action Decider" risks sounding like a framework label. "The Responsible Experience Decider" may blur with Standards-led titles.

Examples are improved but still use a few repeated structures: "imagine a team..." and "before the end of the day." This is acceptable for draft but should be reviewed across all 24 reports.

Section 10 Strengths is clear but sometimes generic. Later polish should make each strength sharper and more pattern-specific.

Section 15 Closing is useful but close in shape across reports. This can wait until the all-report audit.

Rank 4 is generally framed as quieter rather than defective. Evidence-as-rank-4 is handled better than expected: the reports make it a corrective test, not a missing trait.

Evidence, Standards, and Practicality receive distinct treatment, but the distinction is stronger in Decision mechanics, Tightening, Range, and Development than in titles or openings.

The batch follows the approved benchmark style well enough to proceed.

## 11. Judgement-Led Family Rules Extracted

1. Describe Judgement as experience, professional read, pattern recognition, and what the reader notices before everything is fully proven.
2. Do not describe Judgement as instinct, gut feel, mood, impulse, vibe, or unsupported feeling.
3. Vary Judgement-led openings by naming what the reader notices: recurring issue, trust concern, timing problem, people impact, repeated behaviour, or practical risk.
4. Rank 2 must be the main differentiator. Evidence tests the read; Standards checks responsibility; Practicality turns the read into action.
5. Evidence should never become a late justification. When lower-ranked, it should still have power to challenge the first answer.
6. Standards should not become a late scold. It should shape fairness and responsibility while the decision can still change.
7. Practicality should not mean speed only. It should mean useful action, response, first step, or what can be done.
8. Examples should be role-neutral but specific, with varied workplace situations.
9. Titles need to be clearer and less archetypal than this batch currently achieves.
10. Strengths and Closing sections should avoid reusing the same "experience into action" shape across all Judgement-led reports.

## 13. Final Recommendation

These are not blockers for Standards-led drafting.
