Verdict: strong enough to proceed, minor polish recommended later.

The batch handles Standards correctly. Standards is generally presented as responsibility, fairness, trust, consistency, and decisions people can stand behind. The drafts do not collapse into moral superiority, rigidity, rule-following, or compliance-only language.

The main weaknesses are not blockers. Titles rely heavily on "Responsible" and feel less distinctive than the report bodies. Several reports are also leaner than the Evidence-led and Judgement-led batches, which makes some Strengths, Range, and Closing sections feel serviceable rather than benchmark-level.

Proceed to RFA-17D, but schedule Standards-led polish later before the 24-report set is compiled or title-locked.

## 4. Batch-Level Strengths

The batch is readable and practical. The reports use direct sentences, reader-facing language, and concrete decision prompts. They do not sound like theory, therapy notes, or internal authoring guidance.

Standards is handled professionally. The reports describe it through fairness, responsibility, consistency, trust, consequences, and what people can stand behind. They avoid the main Standards-led risks: moralising, perfectionism, bureaucracy, and rule-following for its own sake.

The reports make a useful distinction between responsibility and bureaucracy. Responsibility is not treated as a slow compliance process; it is a decision behaviour that protects people, trust, and future consequences.

Role-neutral examples are present in every report. The examples cover process exceptions, workload changes, people-treatment changes, fairness concerns, operational fixes, and temporary workarounds. They are broad enough for different teams and roles.

Section structure is consistent. Each report preserves the Decision Style section labels and stable IDs from `key-insight` through `closing`.

Development actions are usable. They give concrete review questions such as "What can we do now that still protects the responsibility involved?", "What evidence would be enough to act responsibly?", and "What evidence would make us change this plan?"

The batch avoids most abstract authoring language. It keeps the signal language behavioural: what the reader protects, checks, notices, does, verifies, or brings forward.

## 5. Batch-Level Risks

The reports can sound similar because every report begins with Standards and several openings use close variants of "fair, consistent, and something people can stand behind." This is accurate, but later polish should vary the first page more by rank 2.

"Responsible," "fair," "trust," and "stand behind" are overused across the family. These are correct terms, but the batch would benefit from more variation: consequences, commitments, expectations, precedent, accountability, what people will have to live with, and what the decision teaches others to expect.

Standards does not generally sound moralistic or rigid. The risk is softer: the reports sometimes make Standards sound generically careful rather than sharply decision-specific.

Title duplication and near-duplication are clear risks. Five of six titles begin with "Responsible," and the two Practicality-second titles are especially close.

Examples are useful but formulaic. Most use "imagine a team..." and then walk through the four signals. That works for draft validation, but all-report polish should vary scenario shape.

Section 10 Strengths is clear but sometimes generic. Several sections use the same structure: protect responsibility, add rank 2, check rank 3, bring forward rank 4.

Section 15 Closing is serviceable but similar across the batch. The closings correctly integrate the rank sequence but do not always give a memorable final principle.

Rank 4 is generally framed as quieter rather than missing. This is strongest when Evidence is rank 4: the reports frame it as a corrective check, not a lack of evidence-mindedness. Practicality as rank 4 is slightly more vulnerable to feeling delayed rather than positively useful.

Evidence, Judgement, and Practicality receive distinct treatment, but the distinction is strongest in Decision mechanics, Tightening, and Development. It is weaker in titles and opening paragraphs.

The Practicality-second Standards reports may blur with future Practicality-led reports if RFA-17D does not make the first move clearly action-led. In these reports, responsibility must remain the first trust point.

The Judgement-second Standards reports may blur with Judgement-led reports because titles and opening language use "Experience" strongly. The body copy usually keeps Standards first, but titles need RFA-20A review.

"Decider" vs "Decision Maker" inconsistency continues. This should be resolved in RFA-20A, not here.

## 11. Standards-Led Family Rules Extracted

1. Describe Standards as responsibility, fairness, consistency, trust, consequences, and decisions people can stand behind.
2. Do not describe Standards as moral superiority, rigidity, bureaucracy, perfectionism, compliance, or being difficult.
3. Vary Standards-led openings by naming the specific thing being protected: precedent, workload fairness, trust, commitments, treatment of people, or future consequences.
4. Rank 2 must be the main differentiator. Evidence supports responsibility; Judgement interprets responsibility in context; Practicality turns responsibility into action.
5. Avoid overusing "responsible," "fair," and "stand behind." Use alternatives such as consequences, commitments, expectations, precedent, accountability, and what people will have to live with.
6. Evidence should not become proof after the fact. When lower-ranked, it should still be able to challenge the first answer.
7. Judgement should remain professional read, experience, and noticing likely effects. Do not let it sound like unsupported feeling.
8. Practicality should mean useful action, response, implementation, and what can be done, not speed alone.
9. Examples should be role-neutral but specific, and they should vary beyond "a team needs to..."
10. Titles should be clear and decision-specific, but should not rely on repeating "Responsible" across the whole family.
11. Strengths and Closing sections should avoid generic family summaries. They need rank-specific language.
