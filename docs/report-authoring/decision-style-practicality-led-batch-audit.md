## 4. Batch-Level Strengths

The batch is plain and readable. The reports use short paragraphs, concrete prompts, and familiar workplace language. They are understandable without knowing the signal model.

Practicality is handled professionally. The reports describe it through what can be done, what will work, usable response, first step, implementation, and keeping decisions moving. They avoid the main Practicality-led risks: rushing, shallow execution, short-termism, and "just get it done" framing.

The reports make a useful distinction between useful action and careless action. Practicality is repeatedly strengthened by facts, experience, responsibility, or review, depending on rank order.

Role-neutral examples are present in every report. The examples cover reducing delays, temporary fixes, midweek plan changes, staffing gaps, process changes, and short-term fixes affecting several groups. These are broad enough for different roles and industries.

Section structure is consistent. Each report preserves the Decision Style section labels and stable IDs from `key-insight` through `closing`.

Development actions are practical. They give usable review questions such as "What facts would confirm or challenge this answer?", "Who is affected by this practical answer?", and "Is this action the right size for what we know now?"

Rank 4 is generally framed as quieter, not missing. Standards as rank 4 becomes an earlier responsibility check. Judgement as rank 4 becomes an earlier professional read. Evidence as rank 4 becomes a corrective test that can change the first answer.

The batch avoids most abstract authoring language. It stays close to the reader's actual decision behaviour: what the reader does, checks, notices, protects, explains, and reviews.

## 5. Batch-Level Risks

The reports can sound similar because every report begins with Practicality. Several openings use close variants of "what can be done," "what can work," and "move the situation forward." This is accurate, but later polish should vary the first page more by rank 2.

"Action," "what can be done," "move forward," "response," and "practical" are used heavily across the family. These words are correct, but the batch would benefit from more variation: workable route, usable first step, implementation path, response people can use, realistic option, movement under constraints, or first move that can be reviewed.

Practicality does not generally sound like speed or impatience. The softer risk is that some passages make Practicality feel like generic movement rather than a specific decision standard: what will work in real conditions.

Title duplication and cross-family collision are clear risks. "The Practical Evidence Decision Maker" is close to Evidence-led naming. "The Practical Experience Decision Maker" collides with Judgement-led title territory. "The Practical Responsibility Decision Maker" and "The Practical Standards Decision Maker" collide with Standards-led title territory.

Examples are useful but formulaic. Most use a team scenario and then walk through the four signals in sequence. That is acceptable for first draft, but full-set polish should vary scenario shape.

Section 10 Strengths is clear but sometimes generic. Several reports use the same shape: create movement, add rank 2, check rank 3, bring forward rank 4.

Section 15 Closing is serviceable but similar across the batch. The closings correctly integrate the sequence, but they often repeat "You start with what can be done because decisions need movement."

Evidence, Judgement, and Standards receive distinct treatment, but the distinction is strongest in Decision mechanics, Tightening, Range, and Development. It is weaker in titles and opening paragraphs.

Evidence-second Practicality reports may blur with Evidence-led reports if titles and openings over-emphasise facts. The body copy generally keeps Practicality first.

Judgement-second Practicality reports may blur with Judgement-led reports if titles over-emphasise experience. The body copy usually keeps action first.

Standards-second Practicality reports may blur with Standards-led reports because "responsibility" is a strong title and opening word. The reports need to preserve Practicality as the first move.

"Decider" vs "Decision Maker" consistency is still an all-report issue. Practicality-led titles mostly use "Decision Maker," which is helpful, but RFA-20A should set the final convention across all 24.

## 11. Practicality-Led Family Rules Extracted

1. Describe Practicality as usable action, workable response, implementation, progress, realistic options, and movement under real constraints.
2. Do not describe Practicality as rushing, impatience, cutting corners, low standards, short-termism, or "just get it done" thinking.
3. Vary Practicality-led openings by naming the specific practical need: unblock work, choose a temporary fix, respond under constraints, make a first move, keep implementation realistic, or create a reviewable action.
4. Rank 2 must be the main differentiator. Evidence checks the action; Judgement shapes the action through experience; Standards tests the action for responsibility.
5. Avoid overusing "what can be done," "move forward," "action," and "response." Use alternatives such as workable route, usable first step, implementation path, realistic option, practical route, and reviewable first move.
6. Evidence should not become proof after the action. When lower-ranked, it should still be able to challenge or change the first answer.
7. Judgement should remain professional read, experience, pattern recognition, and noticing likely effects. Do not let it sound like unsupported feeling.
8. Standards should remain responsibility, fairness, consequences, trust, and defensibility. Do not let it sound rigid, moralistic, or compliance-led.
9. Examples should be role-neutral but specific, and they should vary beyond a repeated "team needs a fix" shape.
10. Titles should make the first signal clear without colliding with other families. Avoid titles that overuse signal names or sound like framework labels.
11. Strengths and Closing sections should avoid generic family summaries. They need rank-specific value and more varied final synthesis.
