# Decision Style Remaining 23 Pattern Briefs

## 1. Purpose

This document defines concise authoring briefs for the remaining 23 Decision Style report patterns after the benchmark report was approved.

This is not the full report set. It is not a workbook, import, renderer, runtime, scoring, database, or production-data task. It does not populate `06_Report_Templates.report_body_json`.

The goal is to define accurate, distinct, plain-language pattern directions before full report drafting begins.

## 2. Approved Benchmark Standard

- benchmark pattern: `evidence_practicality_standards_judgement`
- benchmark title: The Evidence-First Decision Maker
- benchmark score: 9.2/10
- benchmark readiness: approved

The approved benchmark standard is plain-language, reader-facing, and practical. Future reports should be clear enough for an intelligent 14-year-old and still credible to a senior professional.

Each full report should:

- open with a plain summary of the reader's decision style
- explain signals as behaviour, not theory
- include at least one role-neutral workplace example
- use "decision style" more than "pattern"
- state practical value and practical risk
- explain how others experience the style
- give clear development actions
- avoid abstract authoring language

## 3. Decision Style Signal Role Rules

### Rank 1

The first trust point. This is what the reader naturally looks to first when deciding.

### Rank 2

The shaping move. This is what the reader uses to turn the first signal into a more complete decision.

### Rank 3

The balancing check. This improves or tests the decision when it is brought in early enough.

### Rank 4

The quieter or underused signal. This may be delayed, less visible, or borrowed from others.

Rank 4 is not a weakness. It is a quieter source of decision intelligence. Full reports should show how the reader can use it earlier without becoming someone else.

Every pattern brief uses this structure:

Example brief heading: `### Pattern [number] — [pattern_key]`

Field definitions:

- working_title: A clear reader-facing title. Avoid abstract metaphor unless it is immediately understandable.
- plain_language_thesis: 2-4 sentences in the benchmark style. It should be understandable without knowing the signal model.
- how_this_reader_decides: Explain the rank sequence in simple behavioural language.
- decision_value: Explain what this decision style helps with at work.
- likely_risk: Explain how the style can go too far.
- others_experience: Explain how people may experience this decision style.
- role_neutral_workplace_example: One simple, broad workplace situation showing the ranked signal sequence in action.
- development_focus: One or two plain development moves.
- differentiation_notes: State how this pattern differs from close neighbours.
- title_risk: Flag if the title may sound too generic, too abstract, or too close to another pattern.
- drafting_notes: Specific notes for whoever writes the full report.

## 6. Briefing Rules by Leading Signal

### Evidence-led reports

Common opening logic: The reader wants the decision to make sense before they commit.

Common value: Steady reasoning, explainable choices, less guesswork.

Common risk: They may wait too long for proof or underuse experience.

Must vary: Do not make every Evidence-led report sound like the benchmark. The second and third signals must change the report meaning.

### Judgement-led reports

Common opening logic: The reader often recognises what is happening before everything can be proven.

Common value: Speed of interpretation, experience, and a human or contextual read.

Common risk: Others may need the reasoning made clearer.

Must vary: Judgement must not sound like guessing, impulse, mood, or unexplained feeling.

### Standards-led reports

Common opening logic: The reader starts by asking what should remain true.

Common value: Fairness, responsibility, trust, consistency, and defensibility.

Common risk: They may become fixed too early or struggle with imperfect trade-offs.

Must vary: Standards must not sound like bureaucracy, moral superiority, or rule-following for its own sake.

### Practicality-led reports

Common opening logic: The reader starts by asking what can actually be done.

Common value: Movement, clarity, usefulness, traction, and realistic choices.

Common risk: They may close too quickly around the workable answer.

Must vary: Practicality must not sound shallow, expedient, or careless.

## 7. The 23 Pattern Briefs

### Pattern 1 — evidence_judgement_standards_practicality

- pattern_key: `evidence_judgement_standards_practicality`
- ranked_signals: Evidence > Judgement > Standards > Practicality
- working_title: The Evidence-and-Experience Decision Maker
- plain_language_thesis: Your decision style starts with facts, then uses experience to understand what those facts may mean. You want the decision to be responsible and something you can stand behind. The risk is that the practical next step may arrive later than other people expect.
- how_this_reader_decides: You first ask what is known. Then you use your professional read to make sense of the situation. After that, you check whether the choice is fair and responsible. Practicality comes last, so the final question is often, "What can we actually do with this?"
- decision_value: This style helps with decisions where facts need interpretation, not just collection. It can create careful, mature decisions that are not rushed into action before the situation is properly understood.
- likely_risk: You may spend too long making sense of the facts and responsibilities before naming the practical move. Others may agree with your reasoning but still need a clearer next step.
- others_experience: People may experience you as thoughtful, serious, and careful with consequences. They may also need you to show the practical path sooner, especially when work is waiting on a decision.
- role_neutral_workplace_example: A team is reviewing a service problem. You want to know what happened, what similar situations have taught you, and what would be fair to the people affected before deciding the next operational step.
- development_focus: Name the practical option earlier, even if it is provisional. Ask, "What is the smallest responsible next move we can take while we keep learning?"
- differentiation_notes: Differs from `evidence_judgement_practicality_standards` because Standards comes before action. Differs from the benchmark because Judgement, not Practicality, interprets Evidence first.
- title_risk: Clear, but close to other Evidence/Judgement titles. Keep subtitle specific in the full report.
- drafting_notes: Do not make this sound slow or academic. The report should show that experience adds useful interpretation before action.

### Pattern 2 — evidence_judgement_practicality_standards

- pattern_key: `evidence_judgement_practicality_standards`
- ranked_signals: Evidence > Judgement > Practicality > Standards
- working_title: The Evidence-and-Read Decision Maker
- plain_language_thesis: Your decision style starts with the facts, then uses experience to read what is really going on. Once the situation makes sense, you look for a practical way forward. The risk is that the responsibility check may come after the decision already feels workable.
- how_this_reader_decides: You first want reliable information. Then you bring in your experience and pattern recognition. Practicality helps you turn that read into action. Standards comes last, so fairness and responsibility may need to be named before the route is already chosen.
- decision_value: This style helps when facts need a human read and the team also needs movement. It can reduce blind action because you do not move until the facts and your experience point in a sensible direction.
- likely_risk: You may trust a strong evidence-and-experience read enough to move forward before fully testing the responsibility side of the choice.
- others_experience: People may trust your ability to understand what is happening. They may also want you to make the fairness or responsibility check more visible before momentum builds.
- role_neutral_workplace_example: A process change has mixed feedback. You review the facts, remember similar changes, choose a practical adjustment, and then need to check whether the change is fair for the people who will carry it.
- development_focus: Bring the Standards question forward. Ask, "Before we act, what would make this fair, responsible, and explainable?"
- differentiation_notes: Differs from Pattern 1 because Practicality comes before Standards. Differs from `judgement_evidence_practicality_standards` because facts, not experience, are the first trust point.
- title_risk: "Read" may be slightly abstract. Consider "The Evidence-and-Experience Decider" if clarity is preferred.
- drafting_notes: Keep Judgement practical and professional. Avoid implying the reader guesses after seeing facts.

### Pattern 3 — evidence_standards_judgement_practicality

- ranked_signals: Evidence > Standards > Judgement > Practicality
- working_title: The Responsible Evidence Decision Maker
- plain_language_thesis: Your decision style starts with facts and quickly checks what would be fair, responsible, and consistent. You then use experience to understand the situation more fully. The risk is that the practical next move may come too late or feel too constrained.
- how_this_reader_decides: You first ask what can be checked. Then you ask what should remain true. Judgement helps you read the context and consequences. Practicality is quieter, so action may wait until the decision feels well supported.
- decision_value: This style helps when decisions need trust, consistency, and a clear explanation. It is useful when people need to know that the choice is based on facts and held to a responsible standard.
- likely_risk: You may hold the decision in review while looking for enough proof and enough responsibility. The practical option can feel secondary until the choice is fully justified.
- others_experience: People may see you as careful and responsible in a grounded way. They may sometimes wish you would name the practical answer sooner.
- role_neutral_workplace_example: A team needs to change a policy. You check the evidence for the problem, test what would be fair and consistent, use experience to spot likely effects, and then work out the practical rollout.
- development_focus: Keep Practicality in the conversation earlier. Ask, "What responsible option can actually be done with the time and resources we have?"
- differentiation_notes: Differs from `evidence_standards_practicality_judgement` because experience comes before implementation. Differs from Standards-led patterns because facts still come first.
- title_risk: Good clarity. It may overlap with Standards-led titles, so the full report must stress Evidence as the starting point.
- drafting_notes: Do not make Standards sound rigid. It is a trust check tied to facts.

## 8. Cross-Pattern Differentiation Notes

Evidence-led patterns differ by what happens after facts are gathered:

- Evidence > Judgement patterns interpret facts through experience before deciding whether to act or check responsibility.
- Evidence > Standards patterns test facts through fairness and responsibility before bringing in experience or action.
- Evidence > Practicality patterns turn facts into action sooner; the third signal determines whether experience or responsibility checks the action.

Judgement-led patterns differ by what the early experienced read does next:

- Judgement > Evidence patterns test the read against facts before acting or checking responsibility.
- Judgement > Standards patterns test the read against responsibility before proof or action.
- Judgement > Practicality patterns turn the read into action early, then need stronger checks from Evidence or Standards.

Standards-led patterns differ by how responsibility becomes a decision:

- Standards > Evidence patterns seek facts after the responsibility frame is clear.
- Standards > Judgement patterns use experience to understand what responsibility means in context.
- Standards > Practicality patterns turn responsibility into action quickly, then need Evidence or Judgement to test it.

Practicality-led patterns differ by how action is checked:

- Practicality > Evidence patterns test action against facts.
- Practicality > Judgement patterns test action against experience.
- Practicality > Standards patterns test action against responsibility.

Common repetition risks when drafting full reports:

- repeating "facts before commitment" across every Evidence-led report
- making Judgement-led reports sound like unexplained hunches
- making Standards-led reports sound strict or moralising
- making Practicality-led reports sound impatient
- using the same role-neutral example structure too often
- describing rank 4 as missing rather than quieter

Pattern pairs likely to blur:

- `evidence_judgement_practicality_standards` vs `evidence_judgement_standards_practicality`: both start Evidence > Judgement; distinguish whether action or responsibility comes third.
- `evidence_standards_practicality_judgement` vs `evidence_practicality_standards_judgement`: both end with Judgement; distinguish whether Standards checks before action or after action.
- `judgement_practicality_evidence_standards` vs `practicality_judgement_evidence_standards`: both combine Judgement and Practicality early; distinguish whether the first move is interpretation or action.
- `standards_evidence_practicality_judgement` vs `evidence_standards_practicality_judgement`: both have Evidence and Standards first; distinguish whether responsibility or proof is the first trust point.
- `practicality_evidence_standards_judgement` vs `evidence_practicality_standards_judgement`: both move between facts and action; distinguish action-first from facts-first.
- `standards_practicality_evidence_judgement` vs `practicality_standards_evidence_judgement`: both have Standards and Practicality first; distinguish responsibility-first from action-first.
