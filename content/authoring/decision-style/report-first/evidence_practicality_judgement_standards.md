# The Practical Evidence Decision Maker

Your decision style is factual, practical, and experience-aware.

You usually want the facts before you commit. Once you have enough to work with, you look for what can actually be done. Then you use experience to test whether that practical option is likely to work in real life.

This makes your decisions clear and useful. The risk is that the fairness or responsibility check may come after the answer already feels sensible.

## 04 — Key insight
<!-- section_id: key-insight -->

You usually start by asking what is known.

You want facts, examples, results, or information that can be checked. You are less comfortable choosing from pressure or preference alone.

Once the facts are clear enough, you look for the practical option. What can be done? What would move things forward? What would work with the time, people, and resources available?

Then you use experience. You ask whether the practical option is likely to work in real life. Have you seen something similar? How might people respond? What could happen after the decision is made?

Your quieter signal is Standards. You may need to bring fairness and responsibility in before the factual, practical, experience-backed answer feels complete.

## 05 — Decision value
<!-- section_id: value -->

This decision style is useful because it turns information into realistic action.

You do not want to debate facts forever. Once there is enough to work with, you look for the option that can actually be used. That helps teams move.

You also do not treat practical action as enough by itself. You use experience to ask whether the option will work beyond the first plan. That helps the decision survive contact with real people and real conditions.

For example, imagine a team has limited time to choose a new process. You check the facts first: what is not working, what evidence supports the change, and what constraints matter. Then you identify the option that can be used now. After that, you draw on experience about how people will respond, before checking whether the choice is fair to the people affected.

That is the value you bring. You help decisions become factual, usable, and realistic.

This is useful when a team needs action but does not want to ignore what experience has taught. You can help people choose a next step and still ask whether that step will hold up in real conditions.

## 06 — Others’ experience
<!-- section_id: others -->

Other people may experience you as clear, practical, and realistic.

They may trust that you have looked at the facts. They may also appreciate that you do not stay stuck in analysis. You want the information to lead to a useful next step.

People may also value your experience. You can often see whether a practical answer is likely to work because you have seen similar choices succeed or fail before.

The part others may need more of is the responsibility check. They may understand why the decision is factual, practical, and experience-backed, but still want to know whether it is fair or something the group can stand behind.

You can make the decision easier to support by saying, "This is the practical option, and here is how we have checked that it is responsible."

That helps others trust the choice more fully.

It also helps people who are affected by the decision. They may not only want to know that the answer works. They may want to know that the effect on them was noticed before the choice was made.

## 07 — Decision mechanics
<!-- section_id: decisions -->

Your usual way of deciding has four steps.

1. **You check the facts.**  
You ask what is known, what can be trusted, and what information should guide the decision.

2. **You look for what can be done.**  
You turn the facts into a practical option. You want something that can move the situation forward.

3. **You use experience to test the option.**  
You ask whether the practical answer is likely to work in real life. You look for patterns, likely reactions, and lessons from similar situations.

4. **You check responsibility.**  
Standards is the quieter signal. You may need to ask earlier whether the choice is fair, consistent, and something you can stand behind.

The strength of this process is that it can move from facts to action without ignoring experience. The risk is that responsibility may be reviewed too late.

## 08 — Explaining the decision
<!-- section_id: communication -->

You may naturally explain the facts and the practical answer first.

That is useful. It lets people understand why you are recommending a specific move. Your experience then helps explain why you think the option will work.

To make the explanation complete, include the responsibility check:

- **What we know:** "Here is the information behind the decision."
- **What we can do:** "Here is the practical option."
- **What experience suggests:** "Here is why I think this will work, and what we should watch."
- **Why this is responsible:** "Here is why the choice is fair and explainable."

The last part matters because a decision can be practical and still create concern. People may need to know how fairness shaped the choice.

When you explain all four parts, others can see the decision as factual, practical, realistic, and responsible.

This matters because your first three signals can sound very convincing. If Standards is not named, people may assume fairness was considered less than usefulness. Say the responsibility check plainly.

## 09 — Judgement under pressure
<!-- section_id: pressure -->

Under pressure, you may move quickly from facts to the practical option.

That can be helpful. If a team needs a clear next step, you may be able to find the option that can work now. You may also use experience to predict whether that option will hold.

The pressure risk is that Standards can be compressed. You may think the answer is strong because it is factual, practical, and experience-backed. But a decision can meet those tests and still be unfair or hard to defend.

For example, a team may need to choose a new process before the next work cycle begins. You check the facts, choose the process that can be used immediately, and use experience to predict how people will respond. Under pressure, you still need to ask whether the change is fair to the people who must carry it.

When pressure is high, ask:

- What do we know?
- What can we do now?
- What does experience say about how this will land?
- If this works, will it still be responsible?

That final question keeps the practical answer from becoming too narrow.

## 10 — Decision strengths
<!-- section_id: strengths -->

**You make decisions actionable.**  
You do not let facts sit unused. You look for what can be done with them.

**You keep action realistic.**  
You use experience to test whether the practical answer is likely to work in real conditions.

**You help teams move.**  
When people are stuck in discussion, you can bring the focus back to the next usable step.

**You can build more trust by bringing Standards forward.**  
When you check fairness earlier, your practical answer becomes easier for others to support.

Together, these strengths make your decision style useful when information needs to become action quickly but not carelessly.

## 11 — Decision tightening
<!-- section_id: tightening -->

This decision style can narrow when the practical answer feels too convincing.

You may check the facts, identify what can be done, and use experience to confirm that the option is likely to work. At that point, the answer can feel complete.

The risk is that responsibility has not had enough influence. Who is affected? Is the change fair? Is the decision consistent with what people were promised? Would you be comfortable explaining it later?

You may also treat Standards as something to check after the answer is chosen, rather than something that should shape the answer.

The warning sign is this: the decision feels factual, doable, and realistic, but the fairness question has not changed anything.

The fix is to ask the Standards question while the answer is still open.

That may feel slower in the moment, but it often saves time later. Decisions that skip responsibility can create follow-up questions, resistance, or repair work that a short early check would have prevented.

## 12 — Wider perspective
<!-- section_id: rank-3-expansion -->

Other people can improve this decision style by testing fairness and impact before action is final.

Invite people who will use or carry the practical option. They can tell you whether it is realistic and whether it creates hidden pressure.

Invite someone who can test responsibility. They can ask who is affected and what would make the choice fair.

Invite someone with different experience. They may see a practical risk that your own experience does not highlight.

Useful questions include:

- Who will have to make this work?
- What would make this practical answer fair?
- What have similar changes taught us?
- What could be true for one group but not another?

These perspectives help you keep action strong without making it too narrow.

They also help you avoid over-relying on your own experience. A practical option may look familiar, but someone closer to the impact may know why this situation is different.

## 13 — Decision range
<!-- section_id: rank-4-expansion -->

This decision style works best when facts can be gathered and a practical response is needed.

It is useful for process changes, resource choices, operational adjustments, and decisions where people need a clear next step.

This style needs adapting when the decision affects fairness, trust, or long-term expectations. In those situations, Standards should come forward before the practical answer feels fixed.

It also needs adapting when the practical option is easy but the impact is uneven. A choice can work for the system and still feel unfair to the people who carry the cost.

Use the style differently by context:

- **High-data decisions:** Let the facts guide the first move.
- **Action-needed decisions:** Identify what can be done.
- **Experience-sensitive decisions:** Ask what similar situations suggest.
- **Fairness-sensitive decisions:** Bring Standards forward.
- **Hard-to-reverse decisions:** Test responsibility before committing.

Range means knowing when a practical answer needs a stronger responsibility check.

In trust-sensitive situations, bring Standards forward before Judgement confirms the practical answer. Otherwise experience may make the action feel even more sensible before fairness has had enough influence.

## 14 — Development
<!-- section_id: development-focus -->

The development work is not to become less practical. It is to make fairness and responsibility part of the practical choice.

**1. Bring Standards forward before the final decision.**  
Ask whether the practical option is fair while it can still be changed.

Why it helps: responsibility shapes the answer instead of reviewing it too late.

Review question: If this works, will it still be responsible?

**2. Test the practical answer with the people affected.**  
Ask how the decision will land, not only whether it can be implemented.

Why it helps: it reveals fairness issues before commitment.

Review question: Who carries the cost of this option?

**3. Separate "works" from "right enough."**  
A practical answer can be strong and still need adjustment.

Why it helps: it stops usefulness from becoming the only test.

Review question: What would make this option easier to stand behind later?

**4. Make responsibility part of the design.**  
Instead of asking whether the final answer is fair, ask how to make the answer fairer while it is still being shaped.

Why it helps: it keeps Standards active without stopping useful action.

Review question: What small change would make this practical answer more responsible?

## 15 — Closing
<!-- section_id: closing -->

At its best, this decision style turns facts into action that can survive real life.

You start with evidence because decisions need support. You look for what can be done because decisions need movement. You use experience because real work is rarely as simple as the plan. And you bring in Standards because useful action still needs to be fair and responsible.

The mature version of this style does not wait until the end to ask whether the answer is responsible. It lets that question shape the answer while there is still time.

Your strongest decisions will come when you can say: this is what we know, this is what we can do, this is what experience tells us, and this is why the choice is fair enough to stand behind.
