# The Experience-and-Evidence Decision Maker

Your decision style often starts with a professional read of what is happening.

You may notice the shape of a situation before every part can be proven. Something feels familiar because you have seen similar patterns before. You then look for facts that can test or support that read.

This makes your decisions thoughtful and careful. You can see meaning early, but you do not want to rely on experience alone. The risk is that the practical next step may need to be made clearer sooner.

## 04 — Key insight
<!-- section_id: key-insight -->

You usually notice something first.

It may be a repeated problem, a human dynamic, a timing issue, or a pattern that reminds you of something you have seen before. You are not guessing. You are using experience to recognise what may be happening before the evidence is complete.

Your next move is to check that read against the facts. What do we know? What can be confirmed? What information supports or challenges what you are noticing?

After that, you ask what would be responsible. You want the decision to be fair, explainable, and something you can stand behind.

Your quieter signal is Practicality. You may understand the situation and the responsibility before you have made the next move concrete enough for others.

## 05 — Decision value
<!-- section_id: value -->

This decision style is useful because it helps people take early experience seriously without treating it as proof.

You may be able to spot that a situation is not as simple as it looks. A recurring issue may not be only a numbers problem. A team conflict may not be only about the visible disagreement. A delay may point to a deeper process problem.

You add value by naming the read, then testing it. That gives the decision both experience and discipline. You do not ask people to accept your view without evidence. You ask what the facts show once the concern has been named.

For example, imagine a team senses that a recurring issue is about more than the visible numbers. You name what experience suggests: the problem may be about handoffs, not effort. You then check the facts, test what would be responsible for the teams involved, and finally define the next step that can be taken.

That is the value you bring. You help others see what may be happening beneath the surface, then you keep the decision honest by checking it.

This is especially useful when people are tempted to treat the visible number as the whole story. You may be the person who says, "The number matters, but it may be showing us a deeper pattern." That kind of contribution can prevent a team from fixing the symptom while leaving the cause untouched.

## 06 — Others’ experience
<!-- section_id: others -->

Other people may experience you as perceptive and careful.

They may value that you can read a situation before everyone else has words for it. You may notice timing, risk, repeated behaviour, or the likely effect on people earlier than others do.

They may also trust that you do not stop at your first read. Because Evidence is second, you are likely to ask what can be checked. This can make your judgement easier for others to work with.

The part others may need more of is the practical next step. They may understand your read. They may accept the facts. They may agree with the responsibility check. But they may still ask, "What do we do now?"

You can help by turning your thinking into a provisional action. You might say, "This is what I think is happening, this is what the evidence supports, this is what would be responsible, and this is the next step I would test."

That gives others both your insight and your direction.

## 07 — Decision mechanics
<!-- section_id: decisions -->

Your usual way of deciding has four steps.

1. **You read the situation.**  
You notice what experience is telling you. You look for patterns, repeated issues, timing, and human factors.

2. **You check the facts.**  
You ask what evidence supports or challenges your read. This keeps your judgement disciplined and easier for others to trust.

3. **You check responsibility.**  
You ask whether the decision is fair, consistent, and something you can stand behind.

4. **You name what can be done.**  
Practicality is quieter in this style. You may need to make the next step visible before the whole situation feels fully worked through.

The strength of this process is that it combines experienced interpretation with proof and responsibility. The risk is that action arrives after a long chain of checks.

## 08 — Explaining the decision
<!-- section_id: communication -->

You may naturally explain a decision by starting with what you have noticed.

That can be useful, but others may need to understand how you got there. If they did not see the same pattern, your read may feel too fast unless you connect it to facts.

A strong explanation has four parts:

- **What I am noticing:** "This looks like a pattern we have seen before."
- **What the facts show:** "Here is the evidence that supports or challenges that read."
- **Why this is responsible:** "Here is what the decision needs to protect."
- **What we can do now:** "Here is the practical step I recommend."

The final part matters because your explanation may otherwise stay in interpretation and responsibility. People need to know what action follows.

When you explain the full chain, your judgement becomes easier to trust. Others can see that it is not random, unsupported, or purely personal.

## 09 — Judgement under pressure
<!-- section_id: pressure -->

Under pressure, your first read may become even stronger.

That can help. If others are overwhelmed by details, you may recognise the pattern quickly. You may know from experience what kind of issue this is and what risk needs attention.

The pressure risk is that you may stay in interpretation and checking while action waits. You may want enough facts and enough responsibility before naming the next move, even when a small step is needed now.

For example, a recurring issue may appear again during a busy week. You recognise that it is probably a handoff problem. You check the evidence and think about what would be fair to the teams involved. Under pressure, the team also needs a practical action to test today.

When pressure is high, ask:

- What am I noticing?
- What facts support or challenge that read?
- What would be responsible?
- What practical step would test whether my read is right?

That last question helps you turn experience into useful movement.

## 10 — Decision strengths
<!-- section_id: strengths -->

**You spot meaning early.**  
You can recognise what may be happening before every detail is proven.

**You test your read.**  
You do not have to rely on experience alone. Evidence helps you check whether your professional read is strong enough.

**You keep responsibility in view.**  
You ask whether the choice is fair and explainable, not only whether it matches your read.

**You can create stronger action when you name it sooner.**  
Practicality helps your insight become useful to others. A small next step can test the read without pretending the whole answer is complete.

Together, these strengths make your decision style valuable in unclear situations where experience notices something before the facts are fully arranged.

## 11 — Decision tightening
<!-- section_id: tightening -->

This decision style can narrow when understanding delays action.

You may read the situation well, then look for evidence, then check responsibility. Those are all useful steps. The risk is that the practical move becomes the last thing anyone hears.

You may also become cautious about naming action because you know the read is not fully proven yet. But action does not have to mean overconfidence. A practical step can be a test.

The warning sign is this: you can explain what is probably happening and why it matters, but the group is still unclear about what to do next.

The answer is to make the next step smaller. You do not need to solve the whole issue. You need one responsible action that tests the read and moves the situation forward.

This is where Practicality becomes a support for Judgement, not a competing signal. A practical step gives your professional read somewhere to go. It lets the team learn whether the read is right.

## 12 — Wider perspective
<!-- section_id: rank-3-expansion -->

Other people can improve this decision style by helping you translate the read into action.

Invite someone close to the work. They can tell you what step is realistic.

Invite someone who can challenge the evidence. They can test whether the facts support your read or whether another explanation is possible.

Invite someone who understands the responsibility side. They can help you see whether the next step would be fair to the people affected.

Useful questions include:

- What else could explain this pattern?
- What would confirm or challenge the read?
- What responsible step could we take now?
- Who needs clarity before we keep investigating?

These perspectives help your judgement become more usable without weakening it.

They also help prevent one common misunderstanding. People may think you are asking for action before the facts are clear, or asking for facts before any action can happen. A wider perspective can help show that the next step is simply a responsible test of what experience is already noticing.

## 13 — Decision range
<!-- section_id: rank-4-expansion -->

This decision style works best when the situation is unclear but familiar.

It is useful for recurring problems, people issues, service concerns, quality drift, and decisions where the visible facts do not explain the whole issue.

This style needs adapting when action is urgent. You may need to name a practical step earlier than feels natural.

It also needs adapting when the decision is low risk. Not every choice needs a long evidence and responsibility chain. Sometimes a small test is enough.

Use the style differently by context:

- **Ambiguous decisions:** Let experience name the likely pattern.
- **Evidence-heavy decisions:** Test your read carefully.
- **Responsibility-sensitive decisions:** Keep Standards visible.
- **Urgent decisions:** Name the smallest useful step.
- **Reversible decisions:** Use action as a way to learn.

Range means knowing when your read needs more proof and when it needs a practical test.

This is the main range skill for this style. Sometimes the decision needs more evidence. Sometimes it needs a small action that creates evidence. Knowing the difference keeps your judgement from becoming either too cautious or too exposed.

## 14 — Development
<!-- section_id: development-focus -->

The development work is not to trust your experience less. It is to make your experience easier to act on.

**1. Translate the read into a test.**  
When you notice a pattern, name one practical step that would test it.

Why it helps: it prevents insight from staying abstract.

Review question: What practical step would test whether my read is right?

**2. Separate evidence from experience.**  
Say what you know and what your experience suggests.

Why it helps: others can trust your judgement without confusing it with proof.

Review question: What facts support this, and what am I reading from experience?

**3. Define the responsible first move.**  
Ask what action can be taken without creating unfair pressure.

Why it helps: it lets the decision move while protecting trust.

Review question: What can we do now that would be fair enough to try?

## 15 — Closing
<!-- section_id: closing -->

At its best, this decision style helps people see what is happening before it is obvious.

You start with experience because repeated situations teach you what to notice. You check the facts because good judgement needs testing. You check responsibility because the decision has to be fair. And you bring in Practicality because insight needs to lead somewhere.

The mature version of this style does not wait for perfect certainty before taking a step. It uses a responsible next move to test the read.

Your strongest decisions will come when you can say: this is what I am noticing, this is what the facts support, this is what would be responsible, and this is the practical step we can take now.
