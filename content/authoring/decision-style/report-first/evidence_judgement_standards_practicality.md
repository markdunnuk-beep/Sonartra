# The Evidence-and-Experience Decision Maker

Your decision style is careful, experienced, and responsibility-aware.

You usually want to understand the facts before you commit. Once you have something solid to work with, you do not move straight to action. You use experience to read what the facts may mean. You then check whether the decision is fair, responsible, and something you can stand behind.

This makes your decisions thoughtful and steady. People can usually see that you have not rushed. The risk is that the practical next step may arrive later than other people expect.

## 04 — Key insight
<!-- section_id: key-insight -->

You usually start by asking, "What do we know?"

You want the decision to rest on facts, examples, results, patterns, or information that can be checked. You are less comfortable choosing because someone is confident, impatient, or persuasive. You want the choice to make sense.

Once you have enough information, your next move is experience. You ask what the facts mean in real life. Have you seen this before? What does the pattern suggest? What is likely happening beneath the obvious details?

After that, you check responsibility. You want the decision to be fair, consistent, and something you can explain later. Only then do you turn strongly toward the practical question: what can we actually do now?

Your quieter signal is Practicality. You may understand the situation well before you have named the next move. The growth edge is to make the practical option visible earlier, even if it is only provisional.

## 05 — Decision value
<!-- section_id: value -->

This decision style is useful at work because it keeps decisions from being based on surface facts alone.

You do not only ask what happened. You ask what it means. That matters because facts often need interpretation. Two teams can look at the same information and reach different conclusions if they do not understand the context behind it.

You also bring responsibility into the process before action takes over. This helps when a choice affects people, trust, service quality, or future expectations. You are likely to ask whether the decision is not only supported, but also fair enough to stand behind.

For example, imagine a team is reviewing a service problem. You want to know what happened, where the issue showed up, and what evidence supports the concern. Then you ask what similar situations have taught you. Is this a one-off issue, or part of a repeated pattern? After that, you check what would be fair to the people affected before deciding the next operational step.

That is the value you bring. You help a group avoid quick answers that do not understand the situation. You make room for facts, experience, and responsibility before the team acts.

## 06 — Others’ experience
<!-- section_id: others -->

Other people may experience you as thoughtful and serious about getting decisions right.

They may trust that you have looked properly at the information. They may also value that you can make sense of what the information means. You are not just collecting facts. You are using experience to understand the situation.

This can be especially helpful when a group is reacting too quickly. You may slow the room down in a useful way by asking, "What do we actually know?" and "What have we seen before that should shape this?"

The part others may find harder is timing. They may agree with your reasoning and still wonder what happens next. If they are waiting on a decision, they may need you to say the practical option sooner.

That does not mean you should rush. It means you may need to separate the direction from the final answer. You can say, "The facts and experience point this way. The responsible next step may be this, even while we keep learning."

When you do that, people get both your care and your direction.

## 07 — Decision mechanics
<!-- section_id: decisions -->

Your usual way of deciding has four steps.

1. **You check the facts.**  
You ask what is known, what can be checked, and what the decision can safely rest on. This protects you from pressure, preference, and unsupported claims.

2. **You use experience to interpret the facts.**  
You ask what the facts mean. You look for patterns, context, and lessons from similar situations. This is where your professional read becomes important.

3. **You check responsibility.**  
You ask whether the decision is fair, consistent, and something you can stand behind. You do not want a choice that is technically supported but hard to defend later.

4. **You name what can be done.**  
Practicality is the quieter part of this style. You may not move to action until the facts, your experience, and the responsibility question all feel clear enough.

The strength of this process is that it creates careful decisions with depth. The risk is that action may wait too long. People may need a provisional next step before every part of the decision feels complete.

## 08 — Explaining the decision
<!-- section_id: communication -->

You can often explain why a decision makes sense because you can show the layers behind it.

Your natural explanation may include the facts and the professional read: "Here is what we know, and here is what I think it means." That is useful, especially when the situation is not simple.

To make the explanation easier for others to follow, include all four parts:

- **What we know:** "Here is the information we can rely on."
- **What experience suggests:** "This looks similar to situations where this was the real issue."
- **Why this is responsible:** "This is the part we need to protect or handle fairly."
- **What we can do now:** "This is the practical next step."

The last part matters for this style. If you leave it too late, people may understand your thinking but still feel unsure about the action.

A strong explanation does not need to pretend everything is certain. It can say, "This is what the facts show. This is what experience suggests. This is the responsible direction. This is the next move we can take while we keep checking."

That kind of explanation gives people confidence and movement.

## 09 — Judgement under pressure
<!-- section_id: pressure -->

Under pressure, you may rely even more on facts and experience.

That can be helpful. If a situation is noisy, you may be able to sort the real issue from the visible reaction. You look for what can be checked, then use experience to understand what might be happening.

The pressure risk is that Practicality may get delayed. You may keep testing the situation while other people need a decision. You may also hold back action because you want the responsibility question to be fully settled.

For example, a team may need to respond to a service problem before the end of the day. You can see the facts, and experience tells you the issue may be deeper than the first complaint. You also want the response to be fair. But the team still needs to know what to do today.

When pressure is high, ask:

- What do we know for sure?
- What does experience suggest?
- What would be responsible?
- What is the smallest next move we can take now?

That last question is important. It lets you act without pretending the whole situation is solved.

## 10 — Decision strengths
<!-- section_id: strengths -->

**You make decisions more thoughtful.**  
You do not stop at the first visible fact. You ask what the information means and what similar situations have taught you.

**You protect decisions from surface logic.**  
Some choices look clear until experience shows what is missing. Your style helps identify those missing pieces before the decision becomes too simple.

**You keep responsibility in view.**  
You ask whether the choice is fair, explainable, and something people can stand behind. This helps protect trust.

**You can create stronger action when you name it earlier.**  
Your practical signal is quieter, but it is still useful. When you name a next step sooner, your careful thinking becomes easier for others to use.

Together, these strengths make your decision style especially useful in complex situations. You help people understand the facts, read the situation, and act responsibly.

## 11 — Decision tightening
<!-- section_id: tightening -->

This decision style can narrow when understanding takes over from movement.

You may keep looking for more information, even when there is enough to choose a direction. You may also keep interpreting the facts through experience, trying to understand the situation fully before naming what should happen next.

Responsibility can add another delay. You may want to know that the decision is fair and defensible before you suggest an action. That is a strength, but it can make the practical step feel late.

The most common tightening is this: you understand the problem better than most people in the room, but the group still does not know what to do.

The answer is not to become less careful. It is to make action provisional. You can say, "Based on what we know and what experience suggests, this is the responsible next step for now."

That keeps your standards high without leaving people stuck.

## 12 — Wider perspective
<!-- section_id: rank-3-expansion -->

Other people can improve this decision style when they help you turn understanding into action.

Involve people who are close to the work. They can tell you what is realistic, what would create friction, and what can be done with the time available.

Involve someone who is comfortable naming a first step. That person does not need to override your care. They can help you translate it.

Also involve people affected by the decision. They can test whether your responsibility check matches the real impact.

Useful questions include:

- What practical step would help now?
- What would be responsible and still doable?
- Who needs clarity before the whole answer is complete?
- What can we try without pretending the issue is fully solved?

Bring these views in before the decision has become too heavy. If you wait until every concern is resolved, action may feel harder than it needs to be.

## 13 — Decision range
<!-- section_id: rank-4-expansion -->

This decision style works best when facts need interpretation and the choice has consequences.

It is useful for service problems, policy questions, sensitive team decisions, quality issues, and situations where a simple answer may miss the real point. You help people avoid acting before they understand.

This style needs adapting when time is short. In urgent situations, you may need to name a practical step before the full picture is clear.

It also needs adapting when a decision is reversible. If the choice can be tested safely, you may not need to hold it until every concern is resolved. You can take a responsible first step and review it.

Use the style differently by context:

- **High-data decisions:** Let the facts start the process.
- **Ambiguous decisions:** Let experience explain what the facts may mean.
- **Human-sensitive decisions:** Keep the responsibility check strong.
- **Urgent decisions:** Name the smallest responsible action.
- **Reversible decisions:** Try a careful first move instead of waiting for the perfect answer.

The aim is to keep your depth while becoming easier to act with.

## 14 — Development
<!-- section_id: development-focus -->

The development work is not to become less careful. It is to make your careful decision process easier for others to use.

**1. Name the practical option earlier.**  
When you are still interpreting the facts, also name the action that might follow.

Why it helps: people can understand the direction while you keep checking.

Review question: What is the smallest responsible next move we can take while we keep learning?

**2. Separate the read from the action.**  
Say what experience suggests, then say what that means for the next step.

Why it helps: it stops your insight from staying abstract.

Review question: If my read is right, what would we do differently?

**3. Use responsibility to shape action, not delay it.**  
Ask what would make the next step fair enough to take now.

Why it helps: it keeps Standards active without making action wait too long.

Review question: What would make this practical step responsible?

## 15 — Closing
<!-- section_id: closing -->

At its best, this decision style gives people more than a quick answer.

You start with facts because decisions need a real basis. You use experience because facts often need interpretation. You check responsibility because the way a decision is made matters. Then you turn that care into a practical step.

The mature version of this style does not wait until everything is complete before acting. It knows how to act carefully while still learning.

Your strongest decisions will come when you can say: this is what we know, this is what experience suggests, this is what would be responsible, and this is the next step we can take now.
