# The Practical Experience Decision Maker

Your decision style is practical, experienced, and focused on what will work in real conditions.

You usually start by asking what can be done. Then you use experience to read whether that action makes sense. You look at people, timing, pressure, and what similar situations have taught you.

Evidence comes in to test the response. Standards is quieter, so the risk is that fairness or responsibility may need to be brought forward earlier.

## 04 — Key insight
<!-- section_id: key-insight -->

You usually begin with the workable response.

You ask what can move the situation forward, what would help, and what can be done with the constraints in front of you.

Your second move is Judgement. You use experience to decide whether the practical answer is likely to work. You may notice timing, people, pressure, adoption, or repeated patterns.

Evidence comes third. You check what can be proven or verified about the action.

Your quieter signal is Standards. You may need to check earlier whether the action is fair, responsible, and defensible.

## 05 — Decision value
<!-- section_id: value -->

This decision style is useful when a situation needs movement and the best response depends on real-world experience.

You can often see what is likely to work before every fact is complete. That does not mean you ignore information. It means experience helps you choose the practical option to test.

For example, imagine a team needs to adjust a plan midweek. You choose the practical adjustment, use experience to predict the impact, check the facts, and then need to ask whether the adjustment is fair.

That is the value you bring. You help people move with a response that is not only possible, but shaped by what has worked before.

This can be especially useful when a purely factual answer is too slow or too thin. The facts may not show how people will respond, where friction will appear, or what kind of action the situation can absorb.

Your decision style helps turn experience into usable movement.

It can also help when the facts are incomplete but the situation cannot wait. You may be able to use experience to choose the action most worth testing, then let evidence confirm or challenge it as the decision develops.

This gives the team a way to move without pretending the first answer is perfect. The action becomes a practical test, not a closed conclusion.

## 06 — Others’ experience
<!-- section_id: others -->

Other people may experience you as practical and seasoned.

They may value that you can read what will work, not only what looks good on paper. They may also appreciate that you can suggest action when others are still trying to understand the situation.

Because Evidence comes third, people may see that you are willing to test the action. You are not asking them to rely only on your experience.

The part others may need more of is the responsibility check. They may need to know that the practical answer is not only realistic, but fair.

You can help by saying, "This is the action I think will work, this is what experience suggests, this is what the facts show, and this is what makes it responsible."

That kind of explanation makes your experience easier for others to use. They can see what is learned from past situations and what still needs to be checked in the current one.

## 07 — Decision mechanics
<!-- section_id: decisions -->

Your usual way of deciding has four steps.

1. **You find the workable move.**  
You ask what can be done and what would move the situation forward.

2. **You read the action through experience.**  
You ask whether the response fits the people, timing, pressure, and history involved.

3. **You check the facts.**  
Evidence tests whether the experienced practical answer has support.

4. **You check responsibility.**  
Standards is quieter in this style. It needs to be named before the action is final.

The strength of this process is realistic action. The risk is that responsibility may arrive late.

## 08 — Explaining the decision
<!-- section_id: communication -->

You may explain decisions through what can be done and why it will likely work.

That can be useful, but others may also need the evidence and the responsibility check.

A strong explanation has four parts:

- **What we can do:** "This is the practical response."
- **What experience suggests:** "This is why I think it will work."
- **What the facts show:** "This is what supports or challenges it."
- **Why it is responsible:** "This is why the action is fair and defensible."

The last part helps people trust the action beyond its usefulness.

## 09 — Judgement under pressure
<!-- section_id: pressure -->

Under pressure, you may rely even more on practical action and experience.

That can help when a situation is moving fast. You may be able to choose a response that fits real conditions before the group gets stuck.

The pressure risk is that Standards gets pushed too late. You may move with an experienced practical answer before asking who is affected and what would make the action responsible.

For example, a team may need to change a plan by the end of the day. You know which adjustment is most likely to work because you have seen similar situations. You check the basic facts. Before acting, you still need to ask whether the change places unfair pressure on one person or group.

When pressure is high, ask:

- What action can work now?
- What does experience suggest?
- What facts should test it?
- Who is affected, and what would make this responsible?

That final question keeps practical experience from becoming too narrow.

## 10 — Decision strengths
<!-- section_id: strengths -->

**You turn problems into action.**  
You look for what can be done instead of staying in uncertainty.

**You make action realistic.**  
Judgement helps you read people, timing, and likely effects.

**You test the response.**  
Evidence helps make sure experience is supported by what is known.

**You can strengthen decisions by naming responsibility earlier.**  
Standards helps make the action fair and easier to stand behind.

Together, these strengths make your decision style useful when progress depends on practical experience.

## 11 — Decision tightening
<!-- section_id: tightening -->

This decision style can narrow when the practical answer feels experience-backed.

You may know what can be done and have a strong read that it will work. Once the facts seem to support it, the answer can feel ready.

The risk is that responsibility becomes a late checkpoint. A response can be realistic and still create an unfair impact.

The warning sign is this: the action feels sensible because experience supports it, but the fairness question has not been asked.

The fix is to bring Standards forward before the action has momentum.

Responsibility does not reduce usefulness. It helps the useful action last.

It also helps people accept the action. A response that works technically may still meet resistance if people think it is unfair or if one group carries too much of the burden.

## 12 — Wider perspective
<!-- section_id: rank-3-expansion -->

Other people can improve this decision style by testing fairness and facts.

Invite someone close to the impact. They can say whether the action feels fair in practice.

Invite someone who can check the information quickly. They can test whether the experienced read has support.

Invite someone with a different experience base. They may have seen a similar action work differently.

Useful questions include:

- Who will carry the impact?
- What facts support this response?
- What has happened when we tried something like this before?
- What would make the action responsible?

These questions help practical experience become a decision others can trust.

They also keep the action from depending too heavily on one person's read. When people can add facts and responsibility checks, the decision becomes easier to support without losing movement.

This is especially useful when the action affects work across teams. A practical answer may look strong from one place and create pressure somewhere else.

They also keep your experience from carrying too much weight on its own. When others can add facts and responsibility checks, the decision becomes stronger without losing movement.

## 13 — Decision range
<!-- section_id: rank-4-expansion -->

This decision style works best when the situation is moving and experience matters.

It is useful for plan changes, operational issues, implementation choices, and moments where the best answer depends on how things actually work.

This style needs adapting when responsibility is central. If the action affects fairness, trust, workload, or precedent, Standards needs to come forward.

Use the style differently by context:

- **Fast-moving decisions:** Let Practicality and Judgement lead.
- **Fact-sensitive decisions:** Check Evidence clearly.
- **Fairness-sensitive decisions:** Bring Standards forward.
- **Hard-to-reverse decisions:** Slow down before acting.
- **People-sensitive decisions:** Ask how the action will land.

Range means knowing when practical experience needs a stronger responsibility check.

In low-risk situations, you may be able to act and review. In high-impact situations, the same decision style needs more deliberate checks before the action spreads.

## 14 — Development
<!-- section_id: development-focus -->

The development work is not to become less practical. It is to make responsibility visible sooner.

**1. Add a Standards checkpoint.**  
Ask whether the experienced practical answer is fair and defensible.

Why it helps: it keeps action from creating avoidable trust problems.

Review question: Who is affected by this practical answer, and what would make it responsible?

**2. Check facts without losing movement.**  
Choose the few facts that matter most.

Why it helps: it lets evidence test the response without stopping progress.

Review question: What facts would confirm or challenge this answer?

**3. Explain experience plainly.**  
Say what you have seen before and why it matters.

Why it helps: it lets others use your judgement instead of treating it as a private read.

Review question: What experience am I using to shape this action?

**4. Add a fairness check while the action is still flexible.**  
Do this before people start organising around the answer.

Why it helps: it gives Standards real influence before momentum builds.

Review question: What would make this practical answer fair enough for the people affected?

## 15 — Closing
<!-- section_id: closing -->

At its best, this decision style creates action that fits real conditions.

You start with what can be done because decisions need movement. You use experience because workable answers depend on context. You check facts because experience still needs support. And you bring in Standards because useful action must also be responsible.

Your strongest decisions will come when practical experience is tested by facts and shaped early by responsibility.
