# The Evidence-and-Action Decision Maker

Your decision style is careful, experienced, and practical.

You usually want the facts before you commit. Once you have enough to work with, you use experience to read what is really going on. Then you look for the practical way forward.

This makes your decisions useful in situations where information needs interpretation and action. The risk is that the fairness or responsibility check may come after the answer already feels workable.

## 04 — Key insight
<!-- section_id: key-insight -->

You usually begin with the facts.

You want to know what happened, what can be checked, and what information is reliable. You are less likely to trust a decision that starts only with opinion, pressure, or a strong feeling.

After that, you use experience. You ask what the facts are pointing to. You may notice that the visible issue is not the whole issue, or that the same pattern has appeared before.

Once the situation makes sense, you look for what can be done. You are not trying to analyse forever. You want a practical answer that can move things forward.

Your quieter signal is Standards. You care about fairness and responsibility, but you may need to bring that check in before the practical answer already feels settled.

## 05 — Decision value
<!-- section_id: value -->

This decision style is useful because it connects facts, experience, and action.

Some people gather information but struggle to interpret it. Others rely on experience but do not show enough evidence. Your style can do both. You want facts first, then a professional read of what those facts mean.

That makes your decisions useful when a team needs to understand a situation and move. You can help people avoid blind action without becoming stuck in review.

For example, imagine a process change has mixed feedback. Some people say it is working. Others say it is making the work harder. You review the facts, look at similar changes you have seen before, choose a practical adjustment, and then need to check whether the change is fair for the people who will carry it.

That is the value you bring. You help a group turn information into a sensible response. You do not need every detail to be perfect. You need enough evidence and enough experience to choose a practical way forward.

This is especially useful when people are divided between "we need more information" and "we need to do something now." You can hold both needs together. You can say what the facts support, what your experience suggests, and what action would make sense from here.

## 06 — Others’ experience
<!-- section_id: others -->

Other people may experience you as perceptive and useful when decisions are unclear.

They may trust that you have checked the facts. They may also value that you can read the situation beyond the first layer of information. You can often say, "This is what the facts show, and this is what I think is really happening."

That can help a group move from confusion to action. You may be especially useful when people have too much information but not enough interpretation.

The part others may need more of is the responsibility check. They may understand why your answer makes sense and still want to know whether it is fair, consistent, or something the group can defend later.

If you make that visible earlier, people do not have to choose between your practical read and their concerns about fairness. They can see how both fit.

This can change the tone of a discussion. Instead of people feeling that they have to challenge your action plan to raise a responsibility concern, they can see that you have already made space for that concern. That makes your recommendation easier to work with.

## 07 — Decision mechanics
<!-- section_id: decisions -->

Your usual way of deciding has four steps.

1. **You check the facts.**  
You ask what is known, what can be trusted, and what the decision can rest on.

2. **You read what the facts mean.**  
You use experience, comparison, and professional judgement. You look for the story behind the information.

3. **You choose the practical move.**  
Once the situation makes sense, you ask what can actually be done. You want action that fits the real situation.

4. **You check responsibility.**  
Standards is the quieter part of this style. You may ask whether the choice is fair or explainable after the practical answer already has momentum.

The strength of this process is that it can move from evidence to interpretation to action. The risk is that a sensible answer can feel complete before you have tested whether it is responsible enough.

## 08 — Explaining the decision
<!-- section_id: communication -->

You may naturally explain decisions by showing what you know and what your experience tells you.

That is helpful. It lets people see that the decision is not random. It is based on information and a professional read of the situation.

To make your explanation stronger, include the responsibility check as well:

- **What we know:** "Here is the information we can rely on."
- **What I think it means:** "Based on experience, this is what seems to be happening."
- **What we can do:** "This is the practical adjustment I recommend."
- **Why this is responsible:** "This is why the choice is fair and explainable."

The last part may not be your first response. You may assume that if the facts, experience, and practical answer make sense, the responsibility is obvious. It may not be obvious to others.

When you explain it directly, people can trust both your read and your action.

## 09 — Judgement under pressure
<!-- section_id: pressure -->

Under pressure, you may move quickly from facts to experience to action.

That can be useful. If a decision is time-bound, you may be able to sort the information, recognise the likely issue, and choose a practical response faster than people who need every detail resolved.

The pressure risk is that Standards can get squeezed. You may assume that the practical answer is good enough because it fits the facts and your experience. But a decision can make sense and still create fairness or trust problems.

For example, a team may need to adjust a process before the next shift starts. You review the feedback, recognise the problem from a similar situation, and choose a practical fix. Under pressure, you may need to pause and ask whether the fix is fair to the people who will have to use it.

When pressure is high, ask:

- What do we know?
- What does experience suggest?
- What can we do now?
- What would make this fair and explainable?

That final question protects the decision from becoming too narrow.

## 10 — Decision strengths
<!-- section_id: strengths -->

**You make information useful.**  
You do not only collect facts. You work out what they mean and how they should shape the decision.

**You bring experienced interpretation.**  
You can recognise patterns, repeated issues, and likely effects before every part is fully proven.

**You move toward action.**  
Once the situation makes sense, you look for what can be done. This helps teams avoid long discussion without movement.

**You can strengthen trust by naming Standards earlier.**  
When you explain why the decision is fair and responsible, your practical answer becomes easier for others to support.

Together, these strengths make your decision style especially useful when a group needs both understanding and movement.

## 11 — Decision tightening
<!-- section_id: tightening -->

This decision style can go too far when the first practical answer starts to feel complete.

You may check the facts, read the situation well, and identify a useful move. At that point, the decision can feel strong. It has information, experience, and action behind it.

The risk is that responsibility has not been tested enough. Who carries the cost? Who is affected? What would make the choice fair? Can it be explained to someone who did not share your read of the situation?

You may also rely on experience in a way that is clear to you but less visible to others. They may need to hear how your read connects to the facts.

The warning sign is this: the decision feels sensible and practical, but you have not asked whether it is fair enough to stand behind later.

Another warning sign is that other people keep asking "what about..." questions after you think the answer is clear. They may not be resisting the decision. They may be trying to bring Standards into a process that has already moved toward action.

## 12 — Wider perspective
<!-- section_id: rank-3-expansion -->

Other people can improve this decision style by testing the responsibility side before action is final.

Invite people who will carry the decision. They can tell you whether the practical answer creates uneven pressure.

Invite someone who can test fairness and consistency. They are not there to block action. They are there to make sure the practical answer is strong enough to explain.

Invite people with different experience from yours. They may read the facts differently and help you avoid over-trusting one interpretation.

Useful questions include:

- Who is affected by this practical answer?
- What would make it fair?
- What assumptions are we making from experience?
- What would we need to explain to people who were not in the room?

The best time to ask these questions is before the answer has too much momentum.

## 13 — Decision range
<!-- section_id: rank-4-expansion -->

This decision style works best when the facts are available, the situation needs interpretation, and action is needed.

It is useful for process changes, team adjustments, service issues, and decisions where the visible information does not explain everything by itself.

This style needs adapting when the decision has a strong fairness or trust element. In those cases, Standards should move earlier. Do not wait until the practical answer is already chosen before asking who is affected.

It also needs adapting when your experience gives you a strong read but the facts are mixed. You may need to slow down enough to separate what is proven from what experience suggests.

Use the style differently by context:

- **High-data decisions:** Let the facts start the process.
- **Experience-heavy decisions:** Say what your read is based on.
- **Action-needed decisions:** Name the practical move.
- **Fairness-sensitive decisions:** Bring Standards forward.
- **Hard-to-reverse decisions:** Test responsibility before momentum builds.

Range for this style means keeping your speed without skipping the check that makes the decision safe to explain.

In low-trust situations, this matters even more. A decision may be factually supported and practically sensible, but if people do not believe it was made responsibly, they may not support it. Bring Standards forward whenever trust is already under strain.

## 14 — Development
<!-- section_id: development-focus -->

The development work is not to trust your experience less. It is to make responsibility visible before action feels settled.

**1. Bring the Standards question forward.**  
Before you recommend the practical answer, ask what would make it fair and explainable.

Why it helps: it keeps action from outrunning responsibility.

Review question: Before we act, what would make this fair, responsible, and explainable?

**2. Show how your experience connects to the facts.**  
Do not assume others can see the same pattern you see.

Why it helps: it makes your read easier to trust.

Review question: What evidence supports my professional read?

**3. Test the practical answer with someone affected.**  
Ask how the answer would land before it becomes final.

Why it helps: it reveals responsibility issues early.

Review question: Who will experience the cost of this choice first?

**4. State the responsibility check in plain words.**  
Do not leave fairness implied. Say what you checked and why it matters.

Why it helps: it makes your practical answer easier to trust.

Review question: What would I need people to hear so they understand this is responsible?

## 15 — Closing
<!-- section_id: closing -->

At its best, this decision style turns facts into useful action through experience.

You start with information because decisions need a basis. You use experience because facts need meaning. You choose a practical answer because decisions need to move. And you bring in Standards because useful action also needs to be fair and responsible.

The mature version of this style does not treat responsibility as the final formality. It makes responsibility part of choosing the action.

Your strongest decisions will come when you can say: this is what we know, this is what experience tells us, this is what we can do, and this is why the choice is fair enough to stand behind.
