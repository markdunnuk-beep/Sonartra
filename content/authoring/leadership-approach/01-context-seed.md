# Leadership Approach Context Seed

## 1. Domain Overview

Leadership Approach describes the way a person is most likely to bring direction, structure, future thinking, and people into leadership work.

In plain terms, it looks at what the person tends to use first when leadership is needed. Some leaders move first through outcomes and decisions. Some move first through structure and reliable process. Some move first through vision and future direction. Some move first through people, trust, and shared commitment.

This domain is practical. It is not a fixed leadership style, status label, personality type, or judgement of seniority. It should help the reader recognise what they naturally bring to leadership, where that approach works well, and what range they may need to add deliberately when the situation asks for more.

Leadership approach may show up when:

- a decision needs to be made
- a team needs clearer direction
- work needs structure or standards
- people need context, confidence, or support
- the current plan needs a future frame
- pressure reveals what the person protects first
- a leader has to balance delivery with trust

The result should help the reader understand how their leadership attention tends to organise itself. It should also help them see where to lean in, where to slow down, and which signal may need to be invited earlier.

## 2. How To Read The Result

The ranked signals describe likely leadership routes. They are not a complete account of the person, a capability rating, or a judgement of leadership worth.

Rank 1 is the clearest starting point. It shows what the person is likely to reach for first when leadership is needed.

Rank 2 is the main shaping influence. It often explains what makes the first signal more effective, credible, balanced, or usable.

Rank 3 is useful support or balancing range. It may not start the approach, but it can help the person adjust, stabilise, or broaden the work.

Rank 4 is the least automatic route. It is not a weakness. It is the leadership condition the person may need to add deliberately when the main approach starts to narrow.

Lower-ranked signals should be framed as range, not deficiency. They may still matter a lot. They may simply be less automatic, less trusted, or used later in the leadership process.

The score shape also matters:

- Concentrated means one signal is clearly stronger. Write rank 1 as the main route without making the approach sound rigid.
- Paired means the top two signals are close. Write them as a real pair, not as rank 1 with a minor helper.
- Graduated means the scores step down clearly. Respect the full order without making it sound like a blunt hierarchy.
- Balanced means all four scores are close. Read rank order lightly and avoid vague wording. Show how the leader can use several routes depending on the situation.

## 3. What The Domain Should Not Do

Do not describe Leadership Approach as:

- a moral judgement
- a seniority score
- a personality label
- a permanent leadership identity
- a complete measure of leadership capability
- a reason to avoid the lower-ranked signals
- a replacement for context, skill, experience, or accountability

Keep the language behavioural. Describe what the reader tends to notice, protect, start, delay, overuse, invite, or miss under pressure.

## 4. Reader Promise

The reader should come away with:

- a clear sense of their most natural leadership route
- recognition of how their top signals work together
- a practical view of what their approach makes easier
- a calm understanding of where it can narrow
- specific ways to use the full range of Results, Process, Vision, and People

## 5. Core Language Standard

Use Plain Behavioural Intelligence:

- clear before clever
- behavioural before abstract
- practical before polished
- calm authority before hype
- British English
- adult tone
- short to medium sentences
- one idea per sentence where possible

Avoid motivational slogans, consultancy language, therapy language, hero language, and abstract nouns carrying the meaning alone.
